2014An Exploration of my Gregorc Learning Style

My thinking style is Concrete Sequential. For better and for worse, I think that the CS description is an accurate reflection of the way I currently think and learn. As a concrete thinker, I take in information based on direct observation, what I see in front of me. I am also a linear thinker, which means that I learn better when I have an outline or plan to work from.

I think that my learning style is well suited to following a structured curriculum with a road map for each week. I enjoy coding challenges, working on my website, and writing blog posts. The biggest struggles for me so far at DBC have been pairing and open-ended assignments such reading and learning about HTML 5, or studying time management techniques. Also, I tend to go down rabbit holes when I don't understand something. I feel like I can't move onto the next concept until I've completely mastered the one I'm currently on.

After reading about concrete sequential learning, it makes sense that assignments where we are expected to research something are more difficult. I think that time boxing and the idea of minimum viable product are strategies I can use to work more efficiently. I can spend a certain amount of time on something, table it and move on the next thing, and then go back if I have time later. This requires learning to be more comfortable with random thinking.

Pairing is difficult for a number of reasons. First, I am shy around people I don't know and tend to overthink social interactions. I don't really know how to fix this other than reminding myself that other people are probably not as critical of me as I am of myself. Second, I am uncomfortable in situations where I don't know the answer, or where I haven't had the chance to review or rehearse material ahead of time. I panic when I'm faced with a new concept that I don't understand immediately, and my instinct is to want get out of the situation as fast as possible and figure it out on my own. I think that checking in with my pair when this happens might be a good strategy. Also, I think that with practice, pairing will get easier.

I would like to work on getting better at thinking conceptually, since it's so important to be able to visualize concepts when solving coding problems. I find pseudocoding and breaking large problems into smaller chunks is extremely helpful for me.

I also want to learn to work better in groups, and not be afraid of looking foolish. I know that being afraid of being wrong, and shutting down when faced with things that are hard to understand are both characteristics of a fixed mindset. Being aware of these tendencies has helped so far. I've also started practicing thinking out loud when I am working by myself, and pretending that I have to explain my thought process to someone else.
